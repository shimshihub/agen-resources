MOTION 1 · CROSS-SELL TO THE FRONTEGG BASE

Customer Expansion GTM Plan

Turn the ~200 existing Frontegg identity customers into the first wave of Agen.co agent-access customers. CSMs already own these relationships, the identity layer is already in place, and the message writes itself: you are already a Frontegg customer, so the secure agent layer is a short step, not a rebuild. This plan covers who we go after, what we put in front of them, how we generate meetings on a modest budget, and how we pay CSMs to make it happen.

1 The play in one paragraph

This is not net-new prospecting. Every account already trusts us with identity and policy, most are mid-market to enterprise B2B SaaS, and many are already discussing AI internally. The job is to give each CSM a short, repeatable reason to open a conversation, a low-friction meeting hook, and a clean handoff to an AE, then pay them in a way that keeps meetings flowing without inviting junk.

2 Segment the base into three tiers

The workspace already exposes the fields we need to tier: Company Co-Tier (company_co_tier), employee count (numberofemployees), CSM owner (csm_owner), and whether an Agen deal is already open. Add one signal per account: is there a known AI or agent initiative. That gives us three treatment groups, each with a different level of effort.

Tier A White-glove

Who: strategic and top-tier accounts, active exec relationship, a known AI initiative, healthy usage.

Treatment: CSM + AE tandem, a pre-built done-for-you MCP as the wow, exec roundtable invite.

Volume: roughly the top 15-20% of the base, worked by name.

Tier B Volume core

Who: healthy mid-market accounts, good relationship, AI on the radar but not committed.

Treatment: free Agent Discovery assessment as the hook, personalized email + in-product + CSM Slack nudge.

Volume: the bulk of the base, the engine of the meeting number.

Tier C Long tail / self-serve

Who: smaller accounts, lighter relationship, or renewal-only touch.

Treatment: automated nurture and an early-bird founding-customer offer. CSM only engages on a reply.

Volume: the remainder, worked with low effort per account.

Do this first. Before any outreach, have each CSM spend 30 minutes tiering their own accounts in /customer-expansion using the filters that already exist (company size, co-tier, Agen-deal status). Meetings come from CSMs who know which 20 accounts to call this week, not from a blast to all 200.

3 Map the account: our champion is rarely the buyer

This is the trap that quietly stalls a cross-sell. Our warm relationship at a Frontegg account usually sits with whoever implemented identity, a developer, a platform lead, or an eng manager. The agent-governance product is bought somewhere else entirely, in security leadership and sometimes IT. So the person we know is a door into the account, not the person who signs. If we pitch the champion as if they were the buyer, the conversation stays friendly and goes nowhere.

Who we have vs who we need

RoleUsually whoTheir job in this motion
Our Frontegg championDeveloper, platform lead, or eng manager who set up identityWarm door and technical validator. Best used to point us to the buyer and make the intro, not to close.
The buyer we needSecurity leadership: CISO, VP or Head of Security. Sometimes CIO or Head of IT.Owns agent governance, risk, and the budget. This is who the meeting needs to reach.
The internal champion for AgenHead of AI, platform security, or the eng lead already feeling the shadow-agent painFeels the problem first and can build the case internally toward the security buyer.

So run two tracks per account

Track A · champion-led intro preferred

Go to the contact we already have and ask one thing: who owns AI and agent security here, and would you introduce me. A warm intro from a trusted colleague beats any cold path to the same exec.

Track B · direct to the buyer parallel

Reach the security leader directly, opening with "you already run Frontegg" and, where the champion is willing, naming them as the referrer. Run this alongside Track A so we are not blocked if the champion goes quiet.

What this changes about "a qualified meeting." A meeting counts when it reaches the buyer or a buyer-side stakeholder, not just any contact we happen to have. Mapping the right person is part of the CSM's qualification, and the accepted-meeting bar in the comp model should reflect that, so we are not paying for friendly meetings with the wrong persona.

Where the rest of the plan has to flex

  • The workspace. The contacts on file in /customer-expansion are often the wrong persona. Each account should surface that gap and prompt the CSM to find the security decision-maker by title through Amplemarket, rather than just listing the contacts we already have. This is a small product tweak worth its own Lovable prompt.
  • Outreach. On any account where our only contact is technical, the first move is an intro-ask to the champion, not a pitch. The founder InMail splits cleanly here: Aviad nurtures the technical champion while Sagi goes to the security exec.
  • Enablement. Give every CSM one discovery question ("who owns agent and AI security for you?") and a short intro-ask script, so the champion-to-buyer handoff is routine rather than improvised.
The reframe in one line: treat the champion as our best route to the buyer, not as the buyer. The relationship is still the advantage, we are just pointing it at the right person.

4 The offer portfolio: hook offers vs conversion offers

You want to experiment with all four offers, so the cleanest way to think about them is by their job. Two offers exist to earn the meeting (low friction, no commitment). Two exist to convert the meeting into a signed expansion (they carry price and terms). Because the Q3 KPI is meetings booked, we lead with the hook offers and hold the conversion offers for the AE conversation.

HOOK Free Agent Discovery assessment

Job: get the meeting. Lowest friction, no purchase, high curiosity.

Why it works: the shadow-agent angle almost always finds agents already connected that nobody knew about. It reframes the call from "buy something" to "let us show you what is already happening."

Best for: all tiers, default hook for Tier B and C.

HOOK Done-for-you MCP

Job: get the meeting with a wow. We pre-build a starter MCP for their product before the call.

Why it works: it proves "we already built it for you" instead of asserting it. Very high open and reply on Tier A.

Best for: Tier A, hand-picked Tier B. Higher effort per account, so reserve it.

CONVERT Free pilot / early-bird

Job: close the expansion. A time-boxed founding-customer cohort with free or heavily discounted access.

Why it works: removes budget as a blocker and creates urgency through a limited cohort and a deadline.

Best for: the AE conversation, any tier that reaches evaluation.

CONVERT Bundle / loyalty pricing

Job: close the expansion by tying the agent add-on to the CIAM renewal or a loyalty bundle.

Why it works: lands cleanly on accounts near renewal and makes the add-on a small delta on an existing spend.

Best for: accounts within ~120 days of renewal, larger Tier A deals.

How to run the experiment

Keep it simple enough that we can actually read the result. Randomize the hook offer within each tier and hold everything else constant, then read reply rate and accepted-meeting rate per hook. Conversion offers are chosen by the AE based on renewal timing and deal size, so we measure those on close rate, not meeting rate.

5 Demand generation: base-only, low spend

These are customers, not strangers, so the highest-yield channels are the ones we already own and cost almost nothing. Paid is a thin retargeting layer on top, not the engine. The whole point of this motion is that the relationship does the work.

1 · CSM shared Slack channels owned

CSMs already sit in shared Slack channels with many customers. A warm, specific note ("saw agent access come up, want me to run our free discovery on your side?") is the single highest-converting first touch we have. Cost: zero.

2 · Personalized CSM email sequence owned

Three touches from the CSM, not marketing: the hook offer, a proof point tied to their stack, and a soft break-up. Tuned to the account AI summary the workspace already generates. Cost: near zero.

3 · In-product placement owned

An in-app message inside the Frontegg admin console for the target accounts: "Turn on secure agent access." We set this up in Appcues ourselves, so no eng time, and it reaches the exact admins we want, where they already work. Cost: near zero.

4 · LinkedIn retargeting of the known 200 low spend

Upload the base as a matched audience and run light reinforcement ads to the buying committee at those exact accounts. This is air cover for the CSM outreach, not a lead source. Small, capped budget.

5 · Lifecycle triggers owned

Fire outreach on renewal windows, QBRs, and any support or product signal touching AI or agents. These moments already have the customer's attention, so the meeting ask lands naturally. Cost: zero, just workflow.

Where the budget actually goes: a modest LinkedIn retargeting cap for the matched audience and LinkedIn Sponsored Messaging for the co-founder InMail at scale (see section 6). Everything else is CSM time, a slice of two founders' time, and a little eng time for the in-product placement. For a base-only motion, spending big on broad paid would be spending against a door that is already open.

6 Co-founder LinkedIn InMail motion

For the top of the base, a personal note from a co-founder beats anything a rep or an ad can send. When Sagi or Aviad reach out directly to an exec at an account that already runs Frontegg, it carries weight no sequence can manufacture: it reads as the people who built the company caring enough to reach out in person. We run this as a focused, personalized layer on Tier A and hand-picked Tier B, not a blast.

Two ways to run it

Manual founder InMail Tier A

Sagi and Aviad send from their own LinkedIn profiles through Sales Navigator, fully personalized to the account. Highest touch, lowest volume. Reserve for named strategic accounts and warm exec relationships.

Sponsored Messaging base scale

LinkedIn Message ads sent to the matched audience with a co-founder set as the sender. Still lands as a personal note in the inbox, but scales across the base without manual sending. This is the low-spend paid line item.

7 CSM compensation model

The motion is book-and-hand-off, so we pay on the accepted meeting as the primary trigger, add a closed-won kicker so CSMs care about quality and outcome, and put guardrails in place so nobody games the meeting count. The numbers below are illustrative starting points to calibrate with RevOps and Finance, not final figures.

$150-250per AE-accepted meeting
$500-1,000closed-won kicker (or 2-3% first-year ACV)
4 / mobaseline accepted-meeting target
1.5×accelerator above target

The three components

ComponentTriggerWhy it is designed this way
Meeting spiff
the engine		Paid per meeting the AE marks accepted (held + fits qualification), not merely booked.Directly rewards the KPI you chose. Tying payout to AE acceptance, not the calendar invite, is what keeps quality honest.
Closed-won kicker
the quality anchor		Flat bonus or a small % of first-year expansion ACV when a CSM-sourced account closes.Without this, CSMs optimize for volume of weak meetings. The kicker makes them care whether the account was actually a fit.
Accelerator + pool
the stretch		Per-meeting rate steps up above the monthly target; a quarterly team pool rewards the whole program hitting its number.Drives stretch from the strong performers and a little team pull so the motion is shared, not solo.

Guardrails (non-negotiable)

  • AE-accepted only. A booked meeting that the AE declines or that no-shows does not earn. Acceptance is the gate.
  • Qualification bar. The account must clear a light fit check (existing customer, right buyer in the room, a real reason to explore). The disqualify path (Not a fit / Not now) earns nothing and is logged in the account notes.
  • 90-day account dedupe. The same account cannot be re-booked for a fresh spiff inside 90 days, so nobody recycles the same logo.
  • Clawback on early churn. The closed-won kicker claws back if the expansion cancels inside the first period, matching standard new-business terms.
Why not a full expansion quota with commission? That model fits a full-cycle owner. Here the CSM hands off before close, so loading them with a revenue quota would pay them on outcomes an AE controls and would slow the meeting engine. A meeting spiff plus a close kicker keeps CSMs pointed at what they actually control (the relationship and the booked meeting) while still giving them upside on the result.

Beyond cash

A cross-sell into your own base is as much about recognition and momentum as it is about the spiff. Layer in a live leaderboard in the program channel, President's Club points for the top bookers, and first pick of the highest-potential expansion accounts for the CSMs who show up. These cost little and often move behavior more than the dollars.

9 90-day rollout

Weeks 0-2 · Set up

Foundations and sign-off

Segment the base into A/B/C inside /customer-expansion. Get comp sign-off from RevOps and Finance. Build the two hook offers (assessment one-pager, done-for-you MCP template). Enable CSMs on the talk track and the workspace. Stand up the leaderboard and the acceptance definition with AEs.

Weeks 3-6 · Pilot

Prove it with 2-3 CSMs on Tier A

Run the done-for-you MCP on hand-picked Tier A accounts and the assessment on a Tier B slice. CSM + AE tandem, with a first batch of manual founder InMail from Sagi and Aviad on a few flagship accounts. Read early reply and accepted-meeting rates. Fix the talk track and handoff before scaling.

Weeks 7-12 · Scale

All CSMs, all channels on

Turn on the email sequence, in-product placement, the LinkedIn retarget, and the co-founder InMail sequence from Sagi and Aviad on Tier A. Run the hook A/B and channel test across the base. Weekly leaderboard, weekly pipeline review of accepted meetings. Start reading which conversion offer closes best by segment.

Ongoing

Double down on what wins

Standardize the winning hook per tier, retire the weaker one, and feed the founding-customer wins back into the base as proof. Revisit the meeting target once real conversion rates are known.

10 Enablement CSMs need on day one

The talk track

Say the company name cleanly, lead with the shadow-agent discovery hook, and ask for a 20-minute assessment, not a demo. Qualify fit before booking. Confirm timezone and read the calendar invite back.

The Agent Discovery one-pager

The free-assessment leave-behind that makes the hook concrete and gives the customer something to say yes to. Doubles as the same-day confirmation asset.

Objection handling

"We are small" → clarify small on what: total headcount, or the team using agents today, which inside a big org is a beachhead. "No AI plans" → that is exactly why the free discovery is useful now.

The workspace itself

How to tier accounts, set the cross-sell stage, log notes on the company object, and Create Opportunity into the Agen pipeline so the handoff to the AE is one click and fully tracked.

The through-line. We are not asking these customers to take a leap. They already trust us with identity, we already built the agent layer, and the first ask costs them nothing but 20 minutes. Pay CSMs for the meeting, give them a hook that opens the door, and keep the whole thing inside the base where the trust already lives.
